# Patristic Data Collection Plan, 0–400 AD

This plan decides **what to collect first**, **what counts as important**, and **how to move from batch to batch without getting lost in the weeds**.

It is designed to work with:
- `app-data.md`
- `data-edit.md`
- `patristic-data-harvest-workflow.md`

---

## North star

Build a dataset that lets the map and claim graph answer questions like these:

- What did major Christian witnesses actually say?
- What doctrines appear as ordinary church life rather than later novelty?
- Where do those doctrines appear geographically?
- Which places, persons, groups, and controversies formed the visible shape of the church?
- How close does the observed early pattern look to Eastern Orthodoxy, Roman Catholicism, evangelical Protestantism, Mennonite positions, or none of the above?

The dataset must be capable of **challenging the thesis**, not merely decorating it.

---

## The governing progression rule

Do **not** progress mainly by:
- decade alone
- doctrine alone
- person alone
- place alone

That creates either fragmentation or tunnel vision.

### Primary progression point: witness cluster campaigns

The basic unit of progress should be a **witness cluster campaign**:

> one anchor witness, or one tightly related cluster of witnesses, checked against a fixed doctrine and geography checklist

This is the best compromise because it:
- keeps batches small
- preserves literary and historical context
- captures relationships and places naturally
- avoids reading the entire century at once
- prevents endless doctrine-mining detached from real texts

### Secondary progression point: controversy campaigns

When a controversy or sect is more important than any one author, use a controversy campaign instead.

Examples:
- Montanism
- rebaptism
- lapsed / penitential discipline
- Arian controversy
- Donatism
- Quartodeciman or Easter disputes

---

## What counts as “important”

A datum is important if it does at least one of these:

1. anchors a core doctrine
2. ties doctrine to a real place
3. ties two or more important people together
4. establishes a major sect or schism
5. marks visible expansion of the church
6. records a persecution, council, martyrdom, or public dispute
7. changes the interpretation of a region on the map

If a fact does none of those, it is probably weed-level for phase 1.

---

## Inclusion heuristics

### People

Include a person when at least one is true:

- direct doctrinal witness
- bishop, martyr, theologian, apologist, major ascetic, major missionary, emperor/state actor, or sect founder
- bridge figure between regions
- key teacher/disciple/correspondent
- major controversy participant
- major source cited by later historians

Do not add every name mentioned in passing.

### Places

Include a place when at least one is true:

- episcopal see
- site of writing, reception, martyrdom, persecution, or synod
- mission or correspondence hub
- stable sect presence
- major expansion marker
- geography needed to interpret a doctrine or schism

Prefer region-level rows when exact cities are uncertain.

### Groups

Include a group when at least one is true:

- distinct doctrinal profile
- geographically relevant presence
- major polemical target
- schismatic or disciplinary significance
- map-visible effect

### Events

Include events that:
- date major persecutions
- date major councils/synods
- date schisms or discipline crises
- date mission/translation moments
- mark imperial turning points affecting church visibility

## The mandatory doctrine set

By the end of the whole campaign, the following must be robustly covered.

### Baptism and initiation
- age/subjects of baptism
- catechumenate
- baptismal regeneration
- rebaptism
- post-baptismal sin and reconciliation

### Eucharist and sacramental realism
- real-presence language
- sacrifice / oblation
- altar language
- communion discipline
- sanctification through material elements

### Oral tradition and apostolic continuity
- tradition language
- rule of faith
- succession
- received custom

### Episcopacy and church authority
- bishop/presbyter/deacon order
- monarchical episcopacy
- collegiality
- schism
- see/chair language

### Mary
- virgin birth
- perpetual virginity / ever-virginity
- typological role where present

### Holy matter / relics / icons / sacred space
- relic treatment
- shrine / tomb / holy place practice
- honor / veneration language
- image practice or anti-image data where present

### Asceticism
- fasting
- celibacy / virginity
- martyr spirituality
- monastic precursors and early monasticism

### Nonresistance / politics / public office
- war / military service
- magistracy
- oath / idolatry / imperial cult
- persecution and obedience tensions

### Other core apostolic-communion patterns
- canon consciousness
- confession / penance
- anointing / laying on hands
- feast/calendar practice where relevant

At minimum, ensure visible coverage for:

- Edessa / Syriac East

## The campaign order

The order below is optimized for doctrinal yield, locality richness, and manageable review.

### Campaign 1 — Apostolic Fathers and immediate post-apostolic church
**Anchor witnesses**
- Didache
- 1 Clement
- Ignatius
- Polycarp
- Barnabas
- Shepherd of Hermas

**Why first**
This is the shortest jump from the NT and gives immediate evidence for:
- church order
- Eucharist
- baptism/initiation
- repentance/discipline
- martyrdom
- city-network spread

**Key places**
- Jerusalem
- Antioch
- Rome
- Smyrna
- Ephesus
- Philippi
- Corinth

**Sign-off condition**
You can see the earliest post-apostolic shape of church order and sacramental life on the map.

---

### Campaign 2 — Early apologists and liturgical witness
**Anchor witnesses**
- Justin Martyr
- Aristides
- Athenagoras
- Melito
- fragments where high-yield

**Why second**
Justin is one of the richest early witnesses for baptism, Eucharist, Sunday assembly, Logos theology, and the church’s public self-understanding.

**Key places**
- Rome
- Samaria / Flavia Neapolis
- Ephesus and Asia Minor links

**Sign-off condition**
Early public Christian worship and self-description are represented by claims, passages, and places.

---

### Campaign 3 — Irenaeus and the anti-gnostic network
**Anchor witnesses**
- Irenaeus
- linked earlier witnesses cited by him where needed

**Why third**
This is the major campaign for:
- apostolic succession
- rule of faith
- tradition
- anti-Valentinian and anti-gnostic evidence
- bishop-centered continuity
- Gaul–Asia Minor–Rome linkage

**Key places**
- Lyons
- Rome
- Smyrna / Asia Minor
- broader Gaul

**Groups**
- Valentinians
- Marcionites
- other gnostic currents

**Sign-off condition**
The dataset can already compare “great church” vs major second-century alternatives.

---

### Campaign 4 — Alexandria before Origen
**Anchor witnesses**
- Clement of Alexandria
- selected Alexandrian material with high doctrinal yield

**Why fourth**
This expands:
- asceticism
- sacramental and moral formation
- tradition and philosophical adaptation
- catechetical urban Christianity

**Key places**
- Alexandria
- Egypt-related network points

**Sign-off condition**
Alexandria is no longer a blank doctrinal zone between NT and Origen.

---

### Campaign 5 — Tertullian and the North African discipline block
**Anchor witnesses**
- Tertullian, grouped by doctrine rather than by complete works

**Why fifth**
Tertullian is too big to read indiscriminately, but crucial for:
- baptism
- Eucharist
- penance
- martyrdom
- military/idolatry/politics
- anti-heretical framing
- Montanist transition

**Key places**
- Carthage
- North Africa
- Rome connections

**Groups**
- Montanists
- heretical targets named in Tertullian

**Sign-off condition**
North African rigor, sacramentality, and church-discipline patterns are visible and not reduced to caricature.

---

### Campaign 6 — Roman-order and church-order controversy campaign
**Anchor witnesses**
- Hippolytus
- Apostolic Tradition material as warranted
- associated Roman dispute material

**Why sixth**
Needed for:
- church order
- ordination
- liturgy
- bishop conflict
- discipline

**Key places**
- Rome

**Sign-off condition**
Rome is no longer represented only by later summaries.

---

### Campaign 7 — Origen and the Alexandrian/Caesarean bridge
**Anchor witnesses**
- Origen
- the exact works/passage clusters with highest doctrinal yield

**Why seventh**
Critical for:
- tradition claims
- infant baptism
- sacramental and interpretive patterns
- explicit “church teaches / does not teach” language
- Alexandria–Caesarea bridge
- teacher/student and travel network inference

**Key places**
- Alexandria
- Caesarea
- Palestine-associated network places

**Sign-off condition**
Origen is represented with direct claims, antitheses, and carefully bounded locality inference.

---

### Campaign 8 — Cyprian, Decian crisis, and rebaptism
**Anchor witnesses**
- Cyprian
- councils/letters around the lapsed and rebaptism disputes

**Why eighth**
High yield for:
- episcopacy
- church unity
- martyr and confessor networks
- baptism
- schism
- North African collegial practice

**Key places**
- Carthage
- North Africa
- Rome

**Groups**
- Novatians / rigorist splits

**Sign-off condition**
The map can now display doctrine, discipline, and schism interacting in real places.

---

### Campaign 9 — Syriac East and eastern expansion
**Anchor witnesses**
- Didascalia-type material where available
- Aphrahat (near the upper boundary)
- high-yield eastern witnesses
- tradition reports used carefully

**Why ninth**
Necessary so the dataset does not become Greco-Latin only.

**Key places**
- Edessa
- Nisibis
- Mesopotamia
- Arabia

**Sign-off condition**
Eastern Christian expansion is visible without pretending every later tradition is equally early.

---

### Campaign 10 — Expansion-edge campaign: Ethiopia, India, Britannia, Arabia
**Anchor witnesses**
- Eusebius and other historians where needed
- early mission traditions with explicit certainty control
- archaeology or episcopal attestations when earlier than narrative tradition

**Why tenth**
This campaign exists to mark the **expanse** of Christianity, not to overstate early details.

**Key places**
- Aksum / Ethiopia
- India
- Britannia
- Arabia
- additional frontier regions as warranted

**Sign-off condition**
The map shows credible outer-range spread with honest certainty levels.

---

### Campaign 11 — Eusebius and the historical registry layer
**Anchor witnesses**
- Eusebius, especially where he preserves earlier data
- selected martyrs and church-history passages

**Why eleventh**
Eusebius is the registry and linkage campaign:
- authors
- works
- sees
- persecutions
- succession chains
- sect notices
- geographic sweep

**Warning**
Do not let Eusebius replace earlier direct witnesses when earlier direct texts survive.

**Sign-off condition**
The network graph becomes much denser and better connected.

---

### Campaign 12 — Fourth-century consolidation to 400
**Anchor witnesses**
- Athanasius
- Basil
- Gregory Nazianzen
- Gregory of Nyssa
- Cyril of Jerusalem
- Ambrose
- John Chrysostom
- Augustine up to the 400 boundary
- selected councils and creedal texts

**Why last**
This is the consolidation pass:
- Nicaea and aftermath
- asceticism explosion
- relic and shrine culture
- Mary data intensification
- public church/state interface
- broad geographic convergence and divergence

**Sign-off condition**
By the end of this campaign, the first 400 years can stand as a coherent visible world.

## How each campaign should run

Each campaign should be completed in this order.

### Step 1 — define the anchor set
Pick the witness cluster.

### Step 2 — define the doctrine checklist
Only 1–3 doctrine families for the current batch.

### Step 3 — define place targets
List the specific cities/regions that should be affected if the batch succeeds.

### Step 4 — define group targets
List sects or communions likely to appear.

### Step 5 — harvest
Use the harvesting workflow.

### Step 6 — canonical ingest
Use the editing workflow.

### Step 7 — sign off
Do not move on until the campaign passes review.

---

## Campaign sign-off checklist

A campaign is complete only when all of the following are true:

### Doctrine
- major direct statements captured
- major antitheses captured
- major presupposed norms captured
- proposition duplicates checked

### Geography
- key places linked
- place rows are not over-precise
- doctrine-to-place inferences are explicitly bounded

### Relationships
- teacher/disciple/correspondent/bishop links added where material
- witness clusters are not isolated nodes

### Groups
- major sects in the campaign captured
- polemical targets not flattened into one generic “gnostic” row when sharper identification is possible

### Events
- persecutions, synods, schisms, martyrdoms, and turning points captured where relevant

### Review
- weak inferences have note coverage
- no large unresolved duplicate set remains
- validator passes

---

## What not to do

### Do not organize the whole project by decade
That creates huge low-signal batches and misses literary coherence.

### Do not organize the whole project by doctrine only
That causes context collapse and encourages quote mining detached from place, person, and controversy.

### Do not treat every witness equally
A short, high-yield witness is often more important than a long diffuse one.

### Do not treat late historical reports as the same kind of evidence as direct earlier testimony
Keep them, but label them correctly.

### Do not force the data to prove your thesis
Your thesis survives only if the contrary evidence is preserved too.

---

## Bias-control rules

These are mandatory because the dataset is apologetically relevant.

1. **Always harvest contrary evidence.**
   If Tertullian leans adult-baptismal in one context, keep that.
   If a witness complicates sacramental realism, keep that.

2. **Differentiate**
   - what is explicitly taught
   - what is assumed
   - what is tolerated
   - what is polemically rejected

3. **Record the opposite side when present.**
   If a controversy exists, add both sides.

4. **Use strong claims only for strong evidence.**
   A passing line is not the same as a doctrinal treatise.

5. **Let uncertainty remain visible.**
   The map should sometimes say “possible” or “mixed.”

---

## Recommended review rhythm

Use this cadence:

- several small batches inside one campaign
- then one campaign review
- then move on

A good normal rhythm is:
- 3–6 micro-batches per campaign
- one sign-off note
- next campaign

That is far better than a giant century-wide data dump.

## End state

The project is in a strong phase-1 state when:

- all mandatory doctrine families have multi-region evidence
- all mandatory groups are visible
- all mandatory expansion zones are represented
- major witness clusters from 70–400 are no longer missing
- proposition-place presence is interesting enough that a user can actually test historical hypotheses on the map

At that point, only then, drill down into finer-grained local people, minor towns, or secondary figures.
