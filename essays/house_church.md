# House Church Movement Rebuttal (AD 33–800)

## Thesis
Early Christian practice cannot be reduced to a single trajectory of "pure house churches" that was later replaced by institutional religion. The historical record shows **overlapping forms**: domestic gatherings, synagogue continuities, urban episcopal structures, and public-facing liturgical communities.

## Why the binary is too simple
1. **Domestic worship is real and early**, especially in periods of legal vulnerability.
2. **Synagogue and civic continuities persist**, including scriptural reading, teaching patterns, and community governance shaped by local imperial conditions.
3. **Institutional development appears early**, not only after imperial favor: letters of Ignatius (early 2nd c.) already assume structured ministries across multiple cities.
4. **Archaeology shows plurality**, not a single model—e.g., Dura-Europos preserves both a Christian house church and a richly decorated synagogue in the same frontier city.

## Mapping implication
Treat each city-decade as a contested evidence zone: track attestation level, polity context, planter traditions vs scholarly dates, and uncertainty statements together.
