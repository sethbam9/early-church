# Infant Baptism in the Early Church

In 253 CE, sixty-six North African bishops assembled under [[person:cyprian-of-carthage|Cyprian of Carthage]] to address a letter from a certain Fidus, who had asked whether an infant should wait until the eighth day after birth before receiving baptism—on the analogy of the eighth-day circumcision required by the Mosaic law. The council's unanimous answer was that no infant should be made to wait: *"We ought not hinder any person from baptism and the grace of God, who is merciful and kind and loving to all. We believe that no one should be excluded from this grace, especially not an infant."* The remarkable feature of [[work:epistula-64-cyprian|this letter]] is what it assumes without argument: that infants are baptized, routinely and without controversy, across the entire North African church. The only question before the council was when to do it.

That sixty-six bishops in 253 CE could address the question of infant baptismal timing as an administrative matter—not whether infants should receive baptism, but how soon—establishes the outer limit of any serious hypothesis that infant baptism was a late development. The practice must have been well-established and universally received before 253. The question is how far back it goes, and the scholarly debate over that question is the substance of this essay.

## The Scholarly Stakes: Jeremias and Aland

The modern form of this dispute was shaped by an exchange between two German scholars. Joachim Jeremias's *Infant Baptism in the First Four Centuries* (1960) assembled the patristic evidence for the antiquity of the practice and argued that household baptisms in Acts, the circumcision typology in Colossians, and Origen's explicit appeal to apostolic tradition establish infant baptism as primitive. Kurt Aland's *Did the Early Church Baptize Infants?* (1961) countered that the earliest unambiguous evidence—[[person:hippolytus-of-rome|Hippolytus]] c. 215, Origen c. 244—establishes only a late second- or early third-century origin, and that Tertullian's objection to hasty infant baptism around 200 CE reflects a *novel* practice being introduced rather than an established one being questioned. Jeremias replied in *The Origins of Infant Baptism* (1963), and the exchange has structured subsequent scholarship, including Everett Ferguson's encyclopedic *Baptism in the Early Church* (2009), which inclines toward Aland's caution on the earliest evidence.

The debate cannot be resolved by appealing to explicit apostolic texts, because no such texts exist for or against infant baptism. It turns on how one reads a set of texts that were written for other purposes and mention baptismal practice only incidentally.

## The New Testament Foundation

No text in the New Testament explicitly commands infant baptism, nor does any text explicitly exclude infants. What we have are household baptism narratives, a theological analogy to circumcision, and the covenantal scope of Peter's Pentecost declaration.

At [[event:pentecost|Pentecost]], [[person:simon-peter|Peter]] declares: *"Repent and be baptized, every one of you, in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins. And you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit. The promise is for you and your children and for all who are far off"* (Acts 2:38–39). The phrase *hymin kai tois teknois hymōn*—"for you and your children"—echoes the covenantal formula of Gen 17:7 and Deut 30:19, where the covenant that God establishes with the present generation explicitly includes their descendants. Luke was a careful writer; the echo is not accidental.

[[work:acts-of-the-apostles|Acts]] records several household (*oikos*) baptisms: the household of [[person:cornelius|Cornelius]] (Acts 10:44–48), the household of [[person:lydia|Lydia]] in [[place:philippi|Philippi]] (Acts 16:15), the Philippian jailer (Acts 16:33), and the household of [[person:stephanas|Stephanas]] in [[place:corinth|Corinth]] (1 Cor 1:16). Ancient *oikoi* were social units that included slaves, freedpersons, and children of all ages. The household was the basic social unit of Greco-Roman society, and it baptized as a unit. The absence of any explicit statement that infants *were* included should be weighed against the equal absence of any statement that they were excluded—and against the fact that the household baptism in Acts 16:33 occurs at midnight, following an earthquake, with no catechetical preparation described for anyone.

[[person:paul|Paul]]'s letter to the Colossians provides the most theologically concentrated background. In [[work:epistle-to-the-colossians|Colossians]] 2:11–12, Paul writes that believers have been circumcised *"with a circumcision not performed by human hands"*—the putting off of the sinful nature—and that this circumcision is *"buried with him in baptism."* The deliberate yoking of circumcision-theology to baptism is not incidental: in the covenant history of Israel, circumcision was the rite of entry into the covenant community, administered to male infants on the eighth day. Paul's typological reading does not mandate infant baptism, but it places baptism precisely in the covenantal location where Israel had placed a rite administered to infants. The theological pressure points in one direction.

## The Second-Century Evidence

**[[person:irenaeus-of-lyons|Irenaeus of Lyon]]** (c. 180) is the second century's most explicit witness to infant baptism. In [[work:against-heresies|Against Heresies]] 2.22.4 he writes that Christ *"came to save all through himself—all, I say, who through him are reborn unto God: infants, and little children, and children, and youths, and older persons."* The phrase "reborn unto God" (*renascuntur in Deum*) is unambiguous second-century baptismal language—the same vocabulary as John 3:5's *"born of water and Spirit"* and Justin's *"regenerated"* in baptism (*1 Apology* 61). [[person:irenaeus-of-lyons|Irenaeus]] explicitly includes *infantes* among those who are reborn through Christ into God.

The [[work:teaching-of-the-twelve-apostles|Didache]] (c. 80–120, likely [[place:antioch|Antioch]] or Syria) prescribes baptism in running water without age restriction, and chapter 9 restricts the Eucharist to the baptized. In a community context where household baptism was the norm, a baptized community with unrestricted access to the Eucharist would include those baptized as infants.

## Tertullian: The Most Important Ambiguous Witness

[[person:tertullian|Tertullian]] of [[place:carthage|Carthage]], writing [[work:de-baptismo|De Baptismo]] c. 198–200, is the patristic figure most commonly cited against infant baptism. The passage deserves careful reading:

> *"According to the circumstances and disposition, and even age, of each individual, the delay of baptism is preferable; principally, however, in the case of little children. Why should the innocent period of life hasten to the 'remission of sins'?... Let them come, then, while they are growing up; let them come while they are learning, while they are learning where they are going; let them become Christians when they have become competent to know Christ."*

[[person:tertullian|Tertullian]] does not say infant baptism is invalid or unprecedented. He says it is *inopportune* (*non necessarium*)—pastorally unwise because sponsors of infant candidates may fail their commitments if the baptized child later lives a sinful life, and because the gravity of post-baptismal sin makes deferral the safer pastoral course. His argument is a discipline argument, not a theological exclusion. More to the point: one does not write a careful pastoral argument against an imaginary custom. The very existence of a sustained argument against hasty infant baptism in [[place:carthage|Carthage]] c. 200 CE is evidence that infant baptism was being practiced and was widespread enough to require a theological response. Aland's reading—that [[person:tertullian|Tertullian]] was reacting to a novel innovation—is possible, but it requires explaining why [[person:tertullian|Tertullian]] offers no argument from novelty, which would have been his strongest point had the practice been recently introduced.

## The Third Century: Apostolic Tradition Named

**[[person:origen-of-alexandria|Origen of Alexandria]]** (184–253) is the most erudite biblical scholar of the early church. Writing in *Homilies on Leviticus* (c. 238–244), he states without qualification: *"The Church received from the apostles the tradition of giving baptism even to infants."* In his *Commentary on Romans* (c. 244–246), he elaborates: *"For this reason the Church had from the apostles a tradition to give baptism even to infants. For those who have been trusted with the divine mysteries knew that there was in all persons the genuine uncleanness of sin, which had to be washed away through water and the Spirit."*

[[person:origen-of-alexandria|Origen]] was a precise philologist who weighed words carefully and whose access to earlier tradition—through Clement of Alexandria, through the catechetical school at [[place:alexandria|Alexandria]], and through his own extensive reading of second-century sources now lost—was greater than that of almost any subsequent patristic scholar. His appeal to apostolic tradition is not a vague sentiment. It is a philological claim about the provenance of a practice, made by someone constitutionally inclined to argue from evidence and against inherited opinion when the evidence warranted. He was also the first to link infant baptism explicitly to the doctrine of original sin—the conviction that every human person, regardless of personal moral choice, participates in the fallen condition inherited from Adam—a linkage that demonstrates the two doctrines were already articulated together a full century and a half before Augustine systematized them.

**[[person:hippolytus-of-rome|Hippolytus of Rome]]** (c. 170–235), in his [[work:apostolic-tradition|Apostolic Tradition]] (c. 215), gives the most detailed pre-Nicene baptismal instruction. Chapter 21 reads: *"Baptize first the children; and if they can speak for themselves, let them do so. Otherwise, let their parents or other relatives speak for them."* [[person:hippolytus-of-rome|Hippolytus]] presents this as the received practice of the Roman church, which he was documenting as a conservative against what he perceived as liturgical innovation by bishop Callistus. The [[work:apostolic-tradition|Apostolic Tradition]] is our clearest pre-Nicene liturgical text, and it makes infant baptism the first and primary case in the baptismal sequence—ahead of adult catechumens.

Aland observes that the sections immediately preceding the baptismal regulations discuss adult catechumens extensively, and argues that "children who can speak for themselves" refers to young children, not neonates. This is a fair textual observation. What it does not explain is why the Roman church of 215 CE would place children first in the baptismal sequence at all, let alone speak of children who *cannot* speak for themselves requiring parental proxies, if infant baptism were a novelty under debate.

## Archaeological Evidence

The **Dura-Europos house church** (c. 230–240 CE) preserves the earliest dedicated Christian baptistery. The font is set into the floor of a small room with a canopy overhead; its dimensions permit immersion for small children and would require a crouched or partially immersed posture for adults. The room is frescoed with the Good Shepherd carrying a sheep (explicitly Paschal and baptismal imagery), the healing of the paralytic, and the Myrrhbearing Women approaching the tomb (a Paschal-resurrection motif continuous with baptism as death and resurrection). Whether infants were baptized at Dura-Europos specifically cannot be determined, but the physical space and its iconographic program are fully consistent with the range of baptismal candidates implied by the contemporary literary sources.

The **Catacombs of Rome** (2nd–4th centuries) contain numerous epitaphs of infants and young children identified as *neophytus* (newly baptized) or bearing Christograms. The quantity and geographical spread of these inscriptions argue that infant membership in the church through baptism was unremarkable from at least the second century onward.

## The Fourth Century: Beyond Controversy

By 325 CE, infant baptism was practiced without controversy across all regions of the church—[[place:rome|Rome]], [[place:carthage|Carthage]], [[place:alexandria|Alexandria]], [[place:antioch|Antioch]], [[place:caesarea|Caesarea]]. No council of the patristic era argued against it in principle. The councils of the fourth and fifth centuries were occupied with Trinitarian and Christological definition; the question of baptismal subjects was never on their agenda because there was nothing to decide. The debates that existed concerned the baptism of those who had lapsed under persecution and sought readmission—not the baptism of infants, which was assumed.

[[person:augustine-of-hippo|Augustine of Hippo]] (354–430)—himself baptized as an adult despite a Christian upbringing—systematized the theology of original sin and tied it explicitly to infant baptism in his anti-Pelagian writings. Augustine was not innovating. He was drawing out implications of what [[person:origen-of-alexandria|Origen]] had already articulated and what the church had practiced for at least two centuries before him. His innovation was not the practice but the precise theological anthropology with which he defended it: the doctrine of *peccatum originale* as a transmitted guilt, not merely a transmitted weakness. That anthropology proved controversial; the baptismal practice it was marshaled to defend was not.

The chronological evidence converges. [[person:origen-of-alexandria|Origen]] appeals to apostolic tradition. [[person:hippolytus-of-rome|Hippolytus]] places infant baptism first in the Roman rite. Sixty-six African bishops in 253 discuss its *timing*, not its validity. The Dura-Europos baptistery is designed for a range of candidates including the very small. The catacombs record baptized infants without surprise. Against this accumulated evidence, the hypothesis that infant baptism was introduced after the apostolic era and before [[person:tertullian|Tertullian]]'s objection—a window of perhaps fifty years—must account for how a novel practice was adopted universally and silently across geographically dispersed and theologically contentious communities without generating a single traceable controversy about its introduction.
