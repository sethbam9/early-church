# Infant Baptism in the Early Church

The question of infant baptism (Latin: paedobaptism) is one of the most contested issues in early church history. This essay surveys the textual and archaeological evidence from the first four centuries, paying close attention to what we can and cannot confidently assert.

## The New Testament Background

The New Testament does not explicitly address infant baptism. Household baptisms — such as those recorded in connection with [[person:cornelius|Cornelius]] (Acts 10) and [[person:lydia|Lydia]] (Acts 16) — have been cited by both sides of the debate. Proponents argue that "household" (oikos) naturally included infants and young children. Critics respond that no infant is ever singled out as a recipient.

[[person:paul-apostle|Paul's]] theology of baptism as a replacement for circumcision (Colossians 2:11–12) is also frequently invoked. If baptism functions as the covenant sign inaugurating membership in the new people of God — analogous to circumcision for Israel — the argument runs that infants born to believers should receive it.

## Second-Century Evidence

The earliest explicit discussion comes from [[person:tertullian|Tertullian]] of [[city:carthage-tunis|Carthage]], writing around AD 200–206. In his treatise *De Baptismo*, Tertullian actually argued **against** hastening baptism for infants and young children, preferring delay until they can understand its significance. The significance of this text is disputed: does Tertullian oppose an existing practice, or merely argue against a practice he views as premature?

[[person:irenaeus|Irenaeus]] of [[city:lugdunum-lyon|Lugdunum (Lyon)]] (c. AD 180) wrote that Christ "came to save all persons" and "passed through every age" — infants, children, youth, adults — arguably indicating that infants are among those who are "regenerated unto God." Many scholars read this as a passing allusion to infant baptism, though it is not explicit.

The [[work:didache|Didache]] (late first or early second century) provides extensive instruction on baptism — including the preference for running water, the option of pouring if needed, and a preparatory fast — but makes no explicit mention of infants. Its silence is sometimes taken as evidence that infant baptism was not yet practiced, though argument from silence is always precarious.

## Origen and the Third Century

[[person:origen|Origen]] of [[city:alexandria-alexandria|Alexandria]] provides arguably the clearest early evidence for the practice. Writing c. AD 244, he states:

> "The Church received from the apostles the tradition of giving baptism even to infants."

Origen treats infant baptism as received apostolic tradition, not a novelty. He also discusses the theological rationale: infants carry the "stain" inherited from Adam and need cleansing. However, Origen's characterization of apostolic tradition must be weighed carefully — he was writing over two centuries after the apostles and his claim may reflect the practice of his own time more than literal apostolic institution.

[[person:cyprian|Cyprian]] of [[city:carthage-tunis|Carthage]] (c. AD 253) and a council of sixty-six bishops explicitly endorsed infant baptism, arguing that grace should not be delayed even a day. The [[event:council-of-carthage-256|Council of Carthage (256)]] addressed whether one must wait until the eighth day (as with circumcision) and concluded no — baptism should not be delayed at all.

## Archaeological Corroboration

The [[archaeology:dura-europos-house-church|Dura-Europos house church]] (c. AD 240) features a baptismal font that scholars have measured and debated. The font's small size has led some researchers to suggest it may have been used for immersing infants or small children, though immersion of adults by pouring is equally plausible. The site remains the earliest surviving purpose-built baptistery.

## Fourth Century and Beyond

By the time of [[person:augustine-of-hippo|Augustine of Hippo]] (b. AD 354), infant baptism was standard across both East and West. Augustine formulated the doctrine of original sin in a way that made infant baptism theologically necessary: without it, infants who died would be consigned to damnation. This position hardened what had previously been a pastoral and covenantal rationale into a soteriological necessity.

The [[event:council-of-nicaea-325|Council of Nicaea (325)]] did not directly address infant baptism, being focused on the Arian controversy, but the widespread assumption of the practice underlies many of its discussions about church membership and discipline.

## Summary

The evidence suggests that infant baptism emerged as a widespread practice during the second and third centuries, with [[person:origen|Origen]] being the first to explicitly claim apostolic warrant for it. Whether the practice predates his writing — perhaps in the sub-apostolic period — remains genuinely uncertain. The trajectory from the New Testament household baptisms through Tertullian's opposition and Origen's defense to Cyprian's council endorsement shows a practice gaining institutional momentum through the third century.

Those who see infant baptism as apostolic in origin point to the household baptism texts, the circumcision-baptism analogy, Origen's claim, and the absence of any ancient protest that it was a novelty. Those who question its antiquity point to Tertullian's opposition, the silence of the Didache and other early sources, and the possibility that Origen's "received from the apostles" was retrospective legitimation.
